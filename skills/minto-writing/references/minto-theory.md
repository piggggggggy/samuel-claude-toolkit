# Minto's Theory and Philosophy

## Core Premise

How Minto assumes the human mind works:

1. **Readers want the answer first.** Writing in the order situation → problem → conclusion is the writer's logic. Readers want the conclusion up front, then want to verify whether it holds.

2. **The brain processes information hierarchically.** Information at the same level groups together, and understanding is faster when higher concepts encompass lower ones.

3. **The question-answer pair is the basic unit.** There is always a question in the reader's mind. Good writing anticipates the flow of those questions and arranges information accordingly.

---

## The Philosophy of the Pyramid Principle

### Why a Pyramid

The pyramid reads top-down. The apex (Answer) first, then supporting points (Key Lines), then detail beneath those.

Why this feels natural: readers keep asking "so what?" The pyramid is a structure that answers that question immediately at every level.

### Two Logics of the Pyramid

**Deductive order**: premise → reason → conclusion
- More abstract toward the top, more concrete toward the bottom
- Connected by "because"

**Inductive order**: multiple cases/evidence → shared conclusion
- Items at the same level each support one higher-level message
- Connected by "therefore"

---

## Minto's View on Persuasion

Minto's conception of persuasion is not about emotion or rhetoric.

**Persuasion = answering the reader's question precisely**

When the reader asks "why?" evidence comes. When they ask "so what?" implications come. When they ask "how?" method comes. Different kinds of questions require different kinds of answers.

---

## Writing and Thinking

One of Minto's central claims: **structuring writing is the same act as structuring thought.**

Unclear writing is a signal that thinking is unclear. The thinking needs to be sorted out first, not the sentences. This is why editing should start at the structural level, not the sentence level.
