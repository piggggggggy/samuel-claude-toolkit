# The SCQA Framework

## Structure

| Element | Meaning | Role |
|---------|---------|------|
| S (Situation) | Stable background the reader already accepts | Establish common ground |
| C (Complication) | Something that disrupts that stability | Create tension |
| Q (Question) | The question C naturally provokes | Set the reader's expectation |
| A (Answer) | The answer to Q — the core message of the piece | Apex of the pyramid |

## On Complication

C is not simply "bad news." It is the addition of a new layer to the stability of S.

- Good C: Reveals something already latent in S. The reader feels "ah, now that you mention it."
- Bad C: Reverse-engineered to justify A. Readers feel the manipulation.
- Bad C: Arrives with no connection to S — it feels imported from outside.

## Four SCQA Variations

**Standard** (most common)
> S → C → Q → A

**Situation type** (reader already knows the problem)
> C → Q → A (S omitted)

**Resolution type** (reader already expects a solution)
> Q → A (S and C omitted)

**Tension type** (to subvert the reader's expectations)
> S → C (A placed later — uses suspense)

## Intro Patterns by Context

### Business / Reporting

**Directive**
- S: We decided to do X
- C: But constraint Y has emerged
- Q: What should we do?

**Request**
- S: You asked me to review X
- C: The review revealed Y
- Q: How should we proceed?

**Problem-framing**
- S: We are currently at X
- C: But the goal is Y (a gap exists)
- Q: How do we close the gap?

### Persuasion / Proposals

**Opportunity**
- S: The market/situation is X
- C: There is an opportunity Y
- Q: How do we capture it?

**Threat**
- S: We are currently at X
- C: Threat Y is approaching
- Q: How do we respond?

**Conflict**
- S: A believes X
- C: But the reality is Y
- Q: Why does this gap exist, and what should we do?

### Explanation / Analysis

**Inquiry**
- S: What is known about X is this
- C: But Y remains unexplained
- Q: Why?

**Evaluation**
- S: We did Y with the goal of X
- C: The result was Z
- Q: What worked and what didn't?
